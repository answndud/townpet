# Product

## Register

product

## Users

TownPet serves Korean pet owners and local community members who need practical information in the middle of real tasks: finding a nearby hospital, checking adoption details, reporting a lost pet, comparing local market posts, or asking neighborhood-specific questions. Many users arrive from mobile, may be unauthenticated, and need to understand trust signals before deciding whether to act.

Secondary users are operators and moderators who need dense, repeatable admin screens for reports, sanctions, health checks, policy changes, and audit trails.

## Product Purpose

TownPet is a policy-first pet community that separates `LOCAL` and `GLOBAL` behavior as an operating rule, not a visual filter. The interface should help users scan local context quickly, understand what is trustworthy, and move from discovery to action without mixing high-risk flows into generic feed patterns.

Success means the product feels like a dependable local information surface: clear enough for first-time guests, structured enough for repeat contributors, and operationally legible enough for moderation and admin work.

## Brand Personality

Calm, structured, neighborly.

The tone is helpful and concrete rather than cute or promotional. TownPet can feel warm because the subject is pets, but the interface should not become toy-like. It should communicate local usefulness, moderation readiness, and everyday reliability.

## Anti-references

- Generic SaaS landing pages with oversized hero metrics, purple-blue gradients, and abstract feature cards.
- Cute pet-app tropes that overuse paw prints, mascots, stickers, or playful colors at the expense of trust.
- Social feeds that hide safety, locality, or policy context behind infinite content.
- Admin dashboards that look decorative but make scan, compare, and triage work slower.
- Nested cards, glassmorphism, gradient text, and one-note palettes.

## Design Principles

1. Make locality visible. The user should understand when they are in a local neighborhood context, a global knowledge context, or an admin operating context.
2. Prioritize structured trust. Category, neighborhood, author status, moderation state, and policy constraints should be easy to scan before decorative details.
3. Keep product UI dense but calm. Feed, search, forms, and admin screens should be efficient for repeated use without feeling cramped.
4. Treat high-risk flows as policy surfaces. Market, lost-found, reports, sanctions, uploads, and guest writes need UI states that expose guardrails instead of hiding them.
5. Improve through existing patterns first. Reuse TownPet tokens, class utilities, and component conventions before introducing new visual primitives.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA for contrast, keyboard navigation, focus visibility, and form error recovery. Korean copy must remain readable at mobile widths. Motion should be subtle, purposeful, and avoid blocking reduced-motion users. Color should not be the only signal for moderation, error, status, or urgency.
